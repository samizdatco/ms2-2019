New York City-funded museums and national art museums.

There are 2,620 art museums across the US, according to the Institute of Museum and Library Services 2018 data files. Who visits these museums and who works there?

What's in the data?

Using publicly available datasets from the National Endowment for the Arts (NEA) audience data, reported findings from two national art museum staff surveys and New York Department of Cultural Affairs (DCLA) grantee staff survey dataset, these visualizations dig into particular findings.

By looking at museum audience demographics, and who the curatorial and leadership staff are, and the artwork collection datasets, information and trends appear. This project aims to look at details in light of larger conversations around contemporary art museums.

Availability of open data limits possible findings to inferences, however by bringing together related sets of information, the aim is to create a richer sense of demographics, who is doing what, and, to an extent, what is changing.

Staff

Who are New York City's publicly funded 'museum' staff - from board to each job type?

A grantee staff survey, developed out of a similar effort to measure the diversity of staff working in US museums conducted the previous year, was commissioned in 2015 by New York City. As the resulting 2016 report rightly pointed out, this is a cautious investigation since, “Quantifying diversity in labor markets is an inherently fraught pursuit, in part because our techniques for recording this data, our nomenclature, our definitions must be necessarily blunt in order to reduce the richness of characteristics we use to categorize ourselves and others (race, ethnicity, gender, age, disability, to name only a few) into discrete categories." These charts here are likewise cautiously drawn.

In order to approximate museums similar to those on the city's Cultural Institutions Group, those classified as museums that have budgets of $999,999+ (in order to select what may more typically be thought of as a museum with collections of works, a building and staff who look after the collection) are depicted.
The NYC DCLA publicly available dataset, with the "Diversity in the New York City Department of Cultural Affairs Community" survey findings from Ithaka S+R and funding partner, The Andrew W. Mellon Foundation, had a big impact on the city’s cultural community.

Following the report, the DCLA initiated a cultural plan, reaching out to “nearly 200,000 New York City residents, from every corner of the city,” to hear priorities, concerns, and ideas from local residents. In July 2017, CreateNYC Plan for the city’s five boroughs, was released. One of the unusual actions initiated was to tie funding to diversity plans, “DCLA will introduce new diversity measures for our city's cultural organizations including collecting data from grantees about the diversity of staff and board members; asking for organizations' approaches to Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI); and in FY 2019 will require DEI plans from the Cultural Institutions Group - 33 members whose institutions are on city-owned property. They receive significant capital and operating support from NYC to help meet basic security, maintenance, administration and energy costs. In return for this support, these institutions operate as publicly-owned facilities whose mandate is to provide cultural services accessible to all New Yorkers.

The national demographic surveys of art museums, one in 2015 and a second in 2018, were undertaken by the same research organization, Ithaka S+R with the assistance of an advisory committee of Association of Art Museum Directors (AAMD) staff and member museum leaders. The 2018 survey compiled responses from 332 art museums, including 136 AAMD institutions that participated in the 2015 survey. Visualizations here are from the report, the datasets are not yet in the public field.

What are art museum staff paid in various job positions? AAMD's 2017 Salary Survey gives the numbers.

Artworks are not categorized in the same way as people are - they are approached by artist, title, date, medium, size, acquisition date, and other categories linked to that object, sometimes the gender of the artist often their nationality is recorded. Here visualizations show nationality of artists to give a flavor of where the makers of the artworks originate and reflect to an extent on the museum collection.
