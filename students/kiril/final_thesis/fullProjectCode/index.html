Tennis is unique from other sports with the fact that there are no "home games" for the players and all tournaments take place in different cities throughout the world. Players on the Women's Tennis Association (WTA) tour typically spend 10+ months of the year on the road, which could include weekly change of climates and altitudes.

All top players compete in the big tournaments, but the choice to go to a smaller one could be dictated by a variety of reasons:

  • tournament is in player's home country/town
  • tournament offers appearance money
  • player is coming back from injury and needs match play to get back in form
  • player suffered an unexpected early-round loss and needs more match play
  • player is chasing the year-end WTA Finals
  • player is just curious about a place

    • Such frequent travel can lead to the accumulatation of fatigue that could negatively influence performance. Medical professionals state that jet lag, sleep interruptions, and altitude changes can result in miscoordinated performance, if there is no adequate time for adjustment. The magnitude of this effect is unknown, but this research evaluates the rate at which the top 30 players earn points per kilometers traveled.

    The author analyzes the full 2018 tennis season of the WTA for the year-end top 30 players, who are in the best position to overcome the drawbacks of travel because of their:

  • top earnings (e.g. first-class travel)
  • available resources (e.g. physiologists)
  • "smart scheduling" (e.g. cherry-picking tournaments)

    • Let's see what it means to be efficient on the WTA tour!

    New Rule of Thumb: In order to be in the top 10, a player generally needs to achieve travel efficiency rate of 1 point for every 20km traveled.

    There are 2 callouts for this efficiency measure:

  • It can only be compared across players, who played a full schedule (case example: Serena Williams appears most efficient but finished the year at No. 16 because she only played in 7 tournaments, which is much below the 17 alloted tournaments that the WTA uses for ranking). A full schedule can be defined as 15 tournaments or more, which is what 90% of the top 30 played in 2018.

  • It does not mimic the rankings one-to-one because of the capped approach to the ranking calculation. Since the ranking system takes the 17 best tournaments, it rewards high peaks in key tournaments and could leave more extensive travel to smaller tournaments unaccounted for. In that sense, losses in smaller tournaments at far-off loactions can have little effect on the rankings if a player has already won big at another stage, but such losses could change the travel efficiency calculation. This is why we see players with good travel efficiency still not make it into the top 10: they did not peak as much in the highest-category tournaments.

    • Overall, this research confirms that the top players maintain very high travel efficiency - they collect more points on average for their travels and are no "journeywomen", but focused professionals playing on a "smart schedule" in order to achieve the maximum returns at the least physical expenditure.

    Because tennis is played on a single tour internationally and not in a conference/regional format, the players have developed very strong habits and defense mechanisms to offset travel-related predicaments from changing time zones, climates, and altitudes. This is why they also rarely bring issues of travel in their interviews, as reported by sports journalist Nick McCarvel (read full interview here).
