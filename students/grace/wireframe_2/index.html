Self-Storage Nation

For over four decades, the self-storage industry has catered to America’s material overflow. About 1 out of 10 Americans will use a self-storage facility at least one point in their lives. Providing an empty space for people’s private possessions the industry has served house and provided a mechanism to hold people’s stuff as they go through the various stages of life (death, divorce, dislocation, displacement, births, and college to name a few). Considering the current state of consumption and living, this thesis examines how the growth of the self-storage industry is representative of America’s consumer patterns, population growth, and migration trends.

Population and Shrinking Living City Dwelling Drives Encourages the Industry

As the population grows, and apartment squarefootage dwingles, Self-Storage becomes the surplas living space for our material goods.

But Self-Storage Thrives in Suburban Markets Too

In suburban markets, average living space is 1,000 ft larger than 1973 (nearly double). Despite this, self-storage continues to grow and become home to these home's material overflow.

Self-Storage Users are as Diverse as Americans

From the super rich with too much material stuff to fit in their homes, to people who have lose their homes, SS is used by both ends of the socio-economic spectrum.

SS is Reflective of E-Commerce Growth

As brick and morters close, SS has often taken those previos locations to now house E-Commerce goods.

Like Starbucks and McDonalds, Self-Storage is a American born creation. However, it's outnumbered it in number of facilities and squarefootage.
